Frequently Asked Questions

If you don't see your question here, give us a call — we're always happy to help.

A dead outlet is most often caused by a tripped GFCI outlet somewhere else on the same circuit, a tripped breaker, or a loose wire connection. Start by checking nearby GFCI outlets (bathrooms, kitchens, garages) — they have a Reset button. If resetting doesn't help, check your breaker panel. If the breaker is fine and the outlet still won't work, there may be a wiring issue that needs a licensed electrician.

A GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter) outlet is a safety device that monitors the flow of current. If it detects even a small imbalance — which can indicate electricity is taking a path through a person — it trips instantly to prevent shock. GFCI outlets are required by code in bathrooms, kitchens, garages, outdoors, and near water. They have two small buttons labeled Test and Reset.

A very brief, small spark when plugging something in is generally normal — it's the electrical current making contact. However, a large, persistent, or repeated spark is not normal and can indicate a loose wiring connection, an overloaded outlet, or a faulty outlet that needs replacement. Don't ignore it — call an electrician to inspect it.

A light switch that feels warm is a warning sign. It can indicate a loose wire connection, an overloaded circuit, a failing switch, or — in older homes — aluminum wiring that needs attention. Some warmth is possible with dimmer switches under heavy load, but any switch that feels hot should be inspected by a licensed electrician promptly.

Occasional flickering when a large appliance starts (like an AC unit or refrigerator) is fairly normal due to a momentary voltage drop. But persistent flickering, flickering in multiple rooms, or flickering that coincides with burning smells or breaker trips points to a more serious issue — loose wiring, a failing connection at the panel, or an overloaded circuit. These warrant a professional inspection.

No — always use a bulb at or below the wattage rating listed on the fixture. Exceeding the rated wattage generates excess heat that can damage the socket, melt insulation on the wiring, and create a fire hazard. If you need more light, consider switching to LED bulbs, which produce the same or more light output at much lower wattage.

A breaker that trips repeatedly is doing its job — but the underlying cause needs to be addressed. Common reasons include an overloaded circuit (too many appliances drawing power), a short circuit in a device or wiring, a ground fault, or a failing breaker. If moving devices to different circuits doesn't solve it, call an electrician. Repeatedly resetting a tripping breaker without finding the cause is a fire risk.

Open your breaker panel and look for a breaker that has moved to the middle position (between ON and OFF) — that's the tripped one. First, turn it fully to the OFF position, then firmly flip it back to ON. If it trips again immediately or won't reset, do not keep resetting it. Unplug devices on that circuit and call us — a breaker that won't hold indicates a wiring or device issue that needs professional attention.

Safety First

When to Call an Electrician Immediately

These signs can indicate a serious and potentially dangerous electrical problem. Don't wait — call us right away.

Burning smells or fishy odors coming from outlets, switches, or your electrical panel

Buzzing or crackling sounds from outlets, walls, or the breaker panel

Discolored or scorched outlet covers — yellowing or black marks indicate heat damage

Shock or tingling sensations when touching an appliance, outlet, or switch
